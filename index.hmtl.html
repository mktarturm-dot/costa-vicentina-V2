The Southwest Alentejo and Vicentine Coast Natural Park

Founded in 1995, this natural park is the largest coastal park in Western Europe. It stretches 105 km between Sines and Cape St. Vincent, covering the municipalities of Sines, Odemira, Aljezur and Vila do Bispo.

The law prohibits any construction that alters the natural landscape — no beachfront resorts, no concrete. The result is beaches that look untouched for centuries: vast sandy shores bordered by vegetation, earth-toned dunes, dramatic cliffs and deserted coves.

But the park is not only coastline. Inland you'll find mountain lagoons, waterfalls, hilltop villages where time slows down and sweeping Alentejo plains. The local cuisine masterfully blends the flavours of the hills and the sea.

It is the absolute opposite of the commercial Algarve — and that is precisely what makes it unforgettable.

🥾 The Rota Vicentina — hiking along the coast

Fishermen's Trail

The most iconic trail. It connects Porto Covo to Odeceixe along ~80 km, hugging the coastline via the same paths local fishermen used to reach the beaches. Breathtaking views around every bend.

Historic Way

The longest route, at ~230 km from Santiago do Cacém to Cape St. Vincent. It crosses the interior of the Natural Park through mountain villages, Alentejo plains and fragrant scrublands before reaching the sea.

Location 01 · Northern Gateway of the Costa Vicentina

Porto Covo

📍 Porto Covo, Sines · Alentejo Coast

Porto Covo is the northern gateway to the Costa Vicentina and one of the most picturesque villages on the entire Portuguese coast. Its central square, lined with whitewashed houses with blue windows and a colonial church, is one of the most iconic images of the Alentejo. It's small, human-scaled and completely genuine.

From the clifftop to the west there is a stunning view of Ilha do Pessegueiro (Peach Tree Island) — an uninhabited islet 400 metres from the shore, with the ruins of a 17th-century fortress. In summer it can be reached by boat; the rest of the year it belongs to cormorants and seagulls.

Porto Covo is also the starting point of the Fishermen's Trail, the most famous route of the Rota Vicentina, which follows the coastline south to Odeceixe over ~80 magnetic kilometres.

Location 02 · The Princess of the Alentejo

Vila Nova de Milfontes

📍 Vila Nova de Milfontes · Odemira

Known as the "Princess of the Alentejo", Milfontes is one of the most beloved coastal towns in Portugal and one of the most complete destinations for a family holiday. It sits at the mouth of the River Mira, which creates a calm, warm lagoon — sheltered from Atlantic waves — perfect for children and swimmers who prefer gentler water.

The town has a relaxed and authentic energy, with excellent terraces, fresh-fish restaurants, a local market and a genuine resident population — something increasingly rare along the Portuguese coastline.

The Milfontes Castle — a 16th-century fortress built to defend the river mouth from Berber pirates — dominates the landscape and now operates as a charming boutique hotel. It's one of the most photographed spots on the coast. There are also three distinct beaches within a 10-minute walk from the town centre: Praia de Milfontes, Praia do Farol and Praia das Furnas.

🏰 Castle 16th C. (now a hotel) 🚣 River Mira — canoeing & kayaking 🏖️ 3 beaches walkable from town 🌊 Surf + calm river swimming 🛶 Boat crossings to the beach

Location 03 · The Beach of the Black Cliffs

Zambujeira do Mar

📍 Zambujeira do Mar · Odemira

Zambujeira do Mar is one of the most striking beaches on the entire Portuguese coast. Nestled between towering black schist cliffs, the beach has an end-of-the-world energy that is completely unique — sheltered from the wind, hidden from the world.

The village that gives access to it is tiny — just one main street with a few cafés and holiday homes — which preserves the place's rare authenticity. The descent to the beach is via a staircase carved directly into the cliff face. The contrast between the dark rocks and the white sand creates a cinematic setting.

Every August, the location is transformed by the MEO Sudowest Festival, one of Portugal's largest open-air music festivals — with 40,000 people over 4 days in this isolated corner of the Costa Vicentina.

Location 04 · A One-of-a-Kind Natural Phenomenon

Odeceixe Beach

📍 Odeceixe · Aljezur · Alentejo/Algarve Border

Odeceixe Beach is a phenomenon with no parallel in Portugal. The River Seixe descends from the hills and flows into the Atlantic right at the beach — splitting it into two worlds within the same stretch of sand: on one side, the fresh, calm and warm river water; on the other, the raw waves of the ocean.

At low tide it is possible to cross the river on foot and reach a small hidden rocky cove to the north. The cliffs have natural pools formed between the rocks — water sandals are recommended to explore them.

This is where the Fishermen's Trail ends. The village of Odeceixe is 2 km away — one of the most charming in the Alentejo, with whitewashed houses, windmills on the hilltop and a mountain-village atmosphere that contrasts wonderfully with the sea below.

Location 05 · The Beach Cradled by Cliffs

Arrifana Beach

📍 Arrifana · Aljezur

Arrifana is one of the most dramatically beautiful beaches in Portugal. Nestled between black basalt cliffs 40 to 60 metres high, the beach has a sheltered and almost sacred energy. The dark basalt contrasts with the golden sand and turquoise waters in a way that looks like a postcard come to life.

At the top of the southern cliff stand the ruins of the Arrifana Fortress — a structure of Arab origin from the 8th century, later adapted by the Portuguese. It is worth climbing up before descending to the beach for a full aerial perspective of the cove; it is one of the most photographed views in western Algarve.

The village of Arrifana on the plateau above has fresh-fish restaurants at reasonable prices and panoramic views over the whole coast. The beach itself is a firm favourite among surfers, with consistent and well-shaped waves throughout the year.

Location 06 · The Biggest and Most Untamed

Bordeira Beach

📍 Carrapateira · Aljezur

At 2.7 km long with dunes reaching 20 metres in height, Bordeira Beach is one of the most vast and spectacular beaches in all of Europe. Its scale is hard to describe — you have to see it to understand. Even in the height of August, the space is so generous it never feels crowded.

The Carrapateira River crosses the beach from end to end, forming shallow lagoons of warmer-than-sea water — perfect for those who prefer not to face the Atlantic waves. The dunes at the back are an attraction in their own right: running down the sandy slopes is an experience enjoyed by all ages.

The Pontal da Carrapateira Trail (11 km, circular, free) is one of the best short walks in all of the Algarve: it departs from the village of Carrapateira, passes through Bordeira and Carrapateira Beach, offering views of both and utterly unique cliff scenery.

📏 2.7 km of beach 🏜️ Dunes up to 20 metres 💧 Warm river lagoons 🥾 Pontal da Carrapateira Trail (11 km) 🪁 Kitesurfing with NW wind

Location 07 · The Cathedral of Portuguese Surf

Amado Beach

📍 Carrapateira · Vila do Bispo

Amado is the quintessential surf beach of the Costa Vicentina. Its direct exposure to the Atlantic, combined with a sandy bottom, delivers well-shaped and consistent waves throughout the year — which is why national and international surf and bodyboard championships are regularly held here.

Several surf schools are set up on the beach and at the entrance, offering wetsuit and board rental for all levels, including children. The beach is lifeguarded in summer and parking is free — something rare at Portugal's best surf beaches.

Even without going in the water, Amado is worth visiting for its remarkable geological formations: the cliff walls display strata from multiple geological eras in different colours and textures, with semi-precious stones sometimes visible in the sand. There is a pleasant café at the entrance serving good pastéis de nata.

Location 08 · The Navigators' Headquarters

Sagres & Sagres Fortress

📍 Sagres · Vila do Bispo · SW tip of the Algarve

Sagres carries immeasurable historical weight. It was here that Prince Henry the Navigator established his famous nautical school in the 15th century — where the explorers who mapped the African coast, discovered Brazil and reached India were trained. Without Sagres, modern history would look very different.

The Sagres Fortress (open daily, 9:30am–5:30pm) is the main monument. Inside, the famous Wind Rose — engraved on the ground with a 43-metre diameter in black and white pebbles — is one of the great historical mysteries of Portugal: nobody knows exactly who built it or when.

On the way to the Cape, Beliche Beach is a hidden beach tucked between cliffs, reached via a steep staircase, with exceptional turquoise waters — one of the best-kept secrets of the western Algarve.

Location 09 · The Final Point of Portugal and Europe

Cape St. Vincent

📍 Cabo de São Vicente · Sagres · Vila do Bispo

The southwesternmost point of Continental Europe — where the land ends and the ocean begins without limit. The Romans called it Promontorium Sacrum, the Sacred Promontory, believing the gods dwelled here. Medieval navigators called it simply "the end of the world" — and from this very point they set sail into the unknown.

The black cliffs plunge 75 metres vertically into the Atlantic. The wind is almost always strong, even in the height of summer. The horizon stretches landless all the way to America. And when the setting sun falls on the ocean, it creates a spectacle of light and colour that is, genuinely, one of the most breathtaking moments Portugal has to offer.

The St. Vincent Lighthouse (built in 1846) is one of the most powerful in Europe — its beam is visible from 60 nautical miles away. At the small market beside the lighthouse, handcrafted woollen jumpers are a popular buy, because even in August the wind at the Cape cuts through you.

🌍 Southwesternmost point of Continental Europe 💡 Lighthouse (1846) — visible for 111 km 🌅 Absolutely unforgettable sunsets 🧣 Artisan wool market 🐦 Major bird migration route

Practical Information

Before you go — the essentials

Everything you need to know to make the most of the Costa Vicentina.

🗓️

Best Time to Visit

May–June and September–October are ideal: guaranteed sunshine, fewer crowds, calmer sea. July–August is hotter but beaches fill up. Winter leaves it almost deserted — and even more dramatic.

🚗

Getting There

A car is essential — there is no adequate public transport between beaches. From Lisbon: ~3h to Porto Covo, ~3.5h to Odeceixe, ~4h to Sagres. The IC4 and N120 cover the entire coast.

🌊

The Atlantic Ocean

This Atlantic is rougher than the south Algarve. Currents can be strong. Always respect the flag system (green/yellow/red) and the lifeguards. Sea temperature 17–21°C in summer.

🏨

Where to Stay

Options for every budget: nature campsites, rural houses, hostels (Sagres/Aljezur) and boutique hotels. Book well in advance for July/August.
→ Hotels guide (Vagamundos)

🍽️

What to Eat

Cuisine that blends fresh fish and seafood with Alentejo flavours. Don't miss: barnacles, clams, sea bass, cataplana stew. In Aljezur try the local sweet potato — a protected designation of origin product.

🥾

Trails & Hikes

The official website has all routes with free GPX maps to download. Trails range from 8 km to 230 km for all levels of fitness.
